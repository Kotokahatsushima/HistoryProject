Brief history of Benguet

In 1572, the spaniard general Juan de Salcedo first ventured into the mountain of Benguet in search of the gold and copper that were said to be coming from the highland region. So from then on, for half a century, expeditions were dispatched in an attempt to subjugate the lands, gold, and people of Benguet. However, in 1664, the fierce and relentless resistance of the Benguet people made the Spanish rulers decide to withdraw attempts at subjugation as well as the pursuit of their land’s gold.

The people of Benguet remained free and independent, but it didn't last when, in 1782, the decree of the tobacco monopoly was issued. The decision of the then Spaniard Gov. Gen. Jose Blanco to send punitive expeditions into areas illegally producing tobacco, clearly since igorots are the lucrative producers and traders of tobacco, as well as reports of raids of the mountain people on the lowlands as well as the flight of fugitives to the mountain areas, caught more attention of the Spanish colonizers. So, punitive expeditions into the Benguet lands were then organized from 1829 to 1833, led by Commandante Guillermo Galvey, which resulted in the burning of villages and settlements.

In 1852, in an effort to map out the mountain territories and contain the illegal trade of tobacco, the “Pais de Igorrotes y Partidas del Norte Pangasinan” was established. This was later divided into four military districts: Benguet, Yamcayan, Abra, and Ifugao. Later on, their Spanish government organized the mountain region into six commandancias, politico militar, namely: Benguet (1846), Lepanto (1852), Bonico (1859), Amburayan (1889), Kayapa (1891), and Kabugao (1891). This commandacias was divided into rancherias.

In 1899, the Katipunan came to Benguet, united the Igorots, and established Benguet under the government of the Republic of the Philippines. Juan Oraa “Ahino” Carino of Tublay was appointed governor of Benguet and chairman of the board.

On August 13, 1908, under Act 1876, Mountain Province was created, consolidating the then province of Benguet, the Commandancia of Quiangan, the sub-province of Kalinga and Apayao, the province of Lepanto-Bontoc, and the sub-province of Amburayan. Benguet became a sub-province of Mountain Province, headed by a deputy governor. So, Benguet remained a sub-province of Mountain Province with the other sub-provinces of Bontoc, Ifugao, and Kalinga-Apayao from 1920 to 1966. As of June 18, 1966, under Republic Act No. 4695, Mt. Province was divided into provinces, namely Benguet, Mt. Province, Kalinga-Apayo, and Ifugao. Thus, Benguet province consists of Atok, Bakun, Bokod, Buguias, Itogon, Kabayan, Kapangan, Kibungan, Tublay, Mankayan, Sablan, Tuba, and La Trinidad, where it is their capital.

In addition, Benguet, together with Mt. Province, Abra, and Kalinga-Apayao, was later separated as provinces, which used to be under Region 1 together with Ifugao under Region 2, and has later compromised the Cordillera Administrative Region or CAR, by virtue of Executive Order 220.

The tribes of Benguet:

1. Ibaloi: The Ibaloi or also known as Nabaloi is an indigenous ethnic group found in the northern Philippines. The “ibaloi” is derived from the term “ipaway” used by the kalanguyas, that means “those who live in the grasslands.”. The Ibalois occupy the southeastern two-thirds of Benguet, particularly the municipalities of Kabayan, Bokod, Sablan, Tublay, La Trinidad, Tuba and Itogon, and the southern portions of Kapangan and Atok (CSG 2003). In the Ibaloi mythology, it is said that their origin came from a couple in mt. Pulog who survived the great flood that was sent by kabunian, a god in their beliefs, to punish the wicked people. This couple gave birth to many children who intermarried, multiplied and came to live in the fertile valleys of Benguet, including Tinek, that became the ancestors of today’s Ibalois. culture and traditions:

Kankana-ey: There are two Kankanaey groups: Most of the northern Kankanaey are located in the southwestern part of Mountain Province and inhabit the municipalities of Besao, Sagada, Tadian, Bauko, and Sabangan. The southern Kankanaey are found in the municipalities of Mankayan, Bakun, Kibungan, Buguias, and the upper half of Kapangan in Benguet. The Kankanaey in Benguet may also be called Benguet Kankanaey to distinguish them from the Benguet Ibaloi, who inhabit the lower half and the most urbanized parts of the province, including the vegetable-growing valley of La Trinidad and the melting-pot city of Baguio. Kalanguya: The Kalanguyas are a small ethno-linguistic group, found in The Philippines, geographically distributed in the mountainous parts of the provinces of Benguet, Pangasinan, Nueva Ecija, Quirino, Ifugao and Nueva Vizcaya. Majority of this group lives in Nueva Vizcaya, with Kapaya as the center where most people reside. The Kalanguyas are also called Ikalahan, Kalanggutan, Keley’I, Mandek’ey and Yatukka (Aguilar 1982). Their name is derived from the cloud or oak-dominated mountain forest (kalasan); Kadasan, as people living in the kalasan (Russell 1992).
